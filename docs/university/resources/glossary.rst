Glossary of Electronic Terms
===============================================================================

A
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**A**
   Abbreviation for ampere, the unit of electrical current.

**Acceptor atoms**
   Trivalent atoms that accept free electrons from pentavalent atoms.

**AC coupling**
   Circuit that passes an AC signal while blocking a DC voltage.

**AC load line**
   Graph representing all possible combinations of AC output voltage and
   current for an amplifier.

**Active component**
   Component that changes the amplitude of a signal between input and output.

**Active filter**
   Filter that uses an amplifier in addition to reactive components to pass
   or reject selected frequencies.

**Active region**
   Region of BJT operation between saturation and cutoff used for linear
   amplification.

**ADC**
   Analog to Digital Converter.

**Aliased signals**
   Signal sampled below the Nyquist Rate so that the frequency content of
   signal is erroneously rearranged.

**Alpha (α)**
   Ratio of collector current to emitter current in a BJT.

**Amplifier**
   Circuit that increases the voltage, current, or power of a signal.

**Amplitude**
   Magnitude or size of a signal voltage or current.

**Analog**
   Information represented as continuously varying voltage or current rather
   than discrete levels.

**Anode**
   The positive electrode or terminal of a device; the P material of a diode.

**Astable**
   Circuit that cannot remain in one state; periodically switches between
   states or oscillates.

**Attenuate**
   To reduce the amplitude of an action or signal.

**Average value**
   Value of voltage or current where the area of the wave above equals the
   area below.

**AWG**
   Arbitrary Waveform Generator or American Wire Gauge.

B
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Bandwidth**
   Width of the band of frequencies between the half power points.

**Barrier potential**
   Natural difference of potential that exists across a forward biased
   PN junction.

**Base**
   Region that lies between the emitter and collector of a bipolar junction
   transistor.

**Base biasing**
   Method of biasing a BJT in which the bias voltage is supplied to the base
   by means of a resistor.

**Beta (β)**
   The ratio of collector current to base current in a BJT.

**Bias**
   DC voltage applied to a device to control its operation.

**Bipolar junction transistor (BJT)**
   Three terminal device in which emitter to collector current is controlled
   by base current.

**Bode plot**
   Graph of gain versus frequency.

**Branch current**
   Portion of total current flowing in one path of a parallel circuit.

**Breakdown voltage**
   Voltage at which the breakdown of a dielectric or insulator occurs.

**Bridge rectifier**
   Circuit using four diodes to provide full wave rectification.

**Buffer**
   Amplifier used to isolate a load from a source.

**Bulk resistance**
   Natural resistance of a P-type or N-type semiconductor material.

**BW**
   Abbreviation for bandwidth.

**Bypass capacitor**
   Capacitor used to provide an AC ground at some point in a circuit.

C
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**CAD**
   Computer aided design.

**Calibration**
   To adjust the correct value of a reading by comparison to a standard.

**CAN**
   Controller Area Network; robust serial communication bus standard popular
   in automotive and industrial applications.

**Capacitance**
   Ability of a capacitor to store an electrical charge.

**Capacitor**
   Electronic component having capacitive reactance.

**Cascaded amplifier**
   Amplifier with two or more stages arranged in a series configuration.

**Cascode amplifier**
   High frequency amplifier made up of a common-source or common-emitter
   amplifier with a common-gate or common-base amplifier in its drain/collector
   network.

**Cathode**
   Negative terminal electrode of a device; the N material in a junction diode.

**Center tapped rectifier**
   Circuit that uses a center tapped transformer and two diodes to provide
   full wave rectification.

**Center tapped transformer**
   Transformer with a connection at the electrical center of a winding.

**Charge**
   Quantity of electrical energy.

**Circuit**
   Interconnection of components to provide an electrical path between two
   or more components.

**Clamp**
   Diode circuit used to change the DC level of a waveform without distorting
   the waveform.

**Class A amplifier**
   Linear amplifier biased so the active device conducts through 360 degrees
   of the input waveform.

**Class B amplifier**
   Amplifier with two active devices biased so that each conducts for
   approximately 180 degrees of the input waveform cycle.

**Class C amplifier**
   Amplifier in which the active device conducts for less than 180 degrees
   of the input waveform cycle.

**Clipper**
   Diode circuit used to eliminate part of a waveform above or below a limit.

**Closed circuit**
   Circuit having a complete path for current flow.

**Closed-loop gain**
   Gain of an amplifier when a feedback path is present.

**CMOS**
   Complementary MOSFET logic; dominates the digital industry.

**Collector**
   Semiconductor region in a BJT through which a flow of charge carriers
   leaves the base region.

**Collector characteristic curve**
   Graph of collector voltage over collector current for a given base current.

**Common base amplifier**
   BJT circuit in which the base connection is common to both input and output.

**Common collector amplifier**
   BJT circuit in which the collector connection is common to both input and
   output.

**Common drain amplifier**
   FET circuit in which the drain connection is common to both input and output.

**Common emitter amplifier**
   BJT circuit in which the emitter connection is common to both input and
   output.

**Common gate amplifier**
   FET circuit in which the gate connection is common to both input and output.

**Common source amplifier**
   FET circuit in which the source connection is common to both input and
   output.

**Common-mode rejection ratio (CMRR)**
   Ratio of op-amp differential gain to common-mode gain.

**Common-mode signals**
   Signals that appear simultaneously at two inputs of an operational amplifier.

**Comparator**
   Op-amp circuit that compares two inputs and provides a DC output.

**Complementary transistors**
   Two transistors, one NPN and one PNP having near identical characteristics.

**Constant current circuit**
   Circuit used to maintain constant current to a load having resistance
   that changes.

**Conventional current flow**
   Concept of current produced by the movement of positive charges towards
   the negative terminal.

**Coulomb**
   Unit of electric charge; a negative coulomb charge consists of
   6.24 × 10\ :sup:`18` electrons.

**Coupling**
   To electronically connect two circuits so that signal will pass from one
   to the other.

**Current**
   Measured in amperes; it is the flow of electrons through a conductor.

**Current amplifier**
   Amplifier to increase signal current.

**Current divider**
   Parallel network designed to divide the total current of a circuit.

**Current feedback**
   Feedback configuration where a portion of the output current is fed back
   to the amplifier input.

**Current mirror**
   Circuit configuration where DC current through one device is replicated
   in another device.

**Cutoff**
   Condition when an active device is biased such that output current is
   near zero or beyond zero.

D
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**DAC**
   Digital to Analog Converter.

**Darlington pair**
   Amplifier consisting of two BJTs with their collectors connected together
   and the emitter of the first driving the base of the second.

**DC**
   Direct current.

**DC load line**
   Graph representing all possible combinations of voltage and current for
   a given load resistor.

**DC offset**
   Change in input voltage required to produce a zero output voltage when
   no signal is applied.

**DC power supply**
   Any source of DC power for electrical equipment.

**Decade**
   Frequency factor of ten.

**Decibel (dB)**
   Logarithmic representation of gain or loss.

**Degenerative feedback**
   Also called negative feedback; a portion of the output of an amplifier
   is inverted and connected back to the input.

**Depletion layer (or region)**
   Area surrounding a PN junction that is depleted of carriers.

**Depletion mode**
   In a FET, an operating mode where reverse gate-source voltage is used
   to deplete the channel.

**Depletion-mode MOSFET**
   MOSFET designed to operate in either depletion mode or enhancement mode.

**Device**
   Component or part.

**Dielectric**
   Insulating material between two plates where an electrostatic field exists.

**Dielectric constant**
   Property of a material that determines how much electrostatic energy can
   be stored per unit volume.

**Dielectric strength**
   Maximum voltage an insulating material can withstand without breaking down.

**Differential amplifier**
   Amplifier in which the output is in proportion to the differences between
   voltages applied to its two inputs.

**Differentiator**
   Circuit in which the output voltage is in proportion to the rate of change
   of the input voltage.

**Diffusion**
   Tendency of conduction band electrons to wander across a PN junction.

**Digital**
   Relating to devices or circuits that have outputs of only two discrete
   levels.

**Digitizing oscilloscope**
   Oscilloscope that uses a high-speed analog-to-digital converter (ADC) to
   measure signals.

**Diode**
   Two terminal device that conducts in only one direction.

**Direct coupling**
   Where the output of one amplifier stage is connected directly to the input
   of a second amplifier or load.

**Direct current (DC)**
   Current that flows in only one direction.

**Donor atoms**
   Pentavalent atoms that give up electrons to the conduction band in an
   N-type semiconductor.

**Doping**
   Process of adding impurity atoms to intrinsic silicon or germanium.

**Duty cycle**
   Fraction of one period in which a signal or system is active.

E
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Electric charge**
   Electric energy stored on the surface of a material.

**Electric field**
   Field or force that exists in the space between two different potentials
   or voltages.

**Electromotive force (emf)**
   Force that causes the motion of electrons due to potential difference
   between two points.

**Electron**
   Smallest subatomic particle of negative charge that orbits the nucleus
   of an atom.

**Electron flow**
   Electrical current produced by the movement of free electrons towards
   a positive terminal.

**Electrostatic**
   Related to static electric charge.

**Emitter**
   Semiconductor region from which charge carriers are injected into the
   base of a BJT.

**Emitter feedback**
   Coupling from the emitter output to the base input of a bipolar junction
   transistor.

**Emitter follower**
   Common collector amplifier with high current gain, high input impedance
   and low output impedance.

**Engineering notation**
   Floating point system in which numbers are expressed as products consisting
   of a number and a power of ten that is a multiple of three.

**Enhancement-mode MOSFET**
   FET in which there are no charge carriers in the channel when the
   gate-source voltage is zero.

**Equivalent resistance**
   Total resistance of all the individual resistances in a circuit.

F
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Fall time**
   Time it takes the falling edge of a pulse to go from 90% of peak voltage
   to 10% of peak voltage.

**Farad**
   Basic unit of capacitance.

**Feedback**
   Portion of the output signal of an amplifier which is connected back to
   the input.

**Feedback amplifier**
   Amplifier with an external signal path from its output back to its input.

**Field effect transistor (FET)**
   Voltage controlled transistor in which the source to drain conduction is
   controlled by gate to source voltage.

**Filter**
   Network consisting of capacitors, resistors and/or inductors used to pass
   certain frequencies and block others.

**Flip-flop**
   Digital circuit that flips or toggles between two stable states (bistable).

**Forward bias**
   PN junction bias which allows current to flow through the junction.

**Free electrons**
   Electrons that are not in any orbit around a nucleus.

**Frequency-domain analysis**
   Method of representing a waveform by plotting its amplitude against
   frequency.

**Frequency response**
   Indication of how well a circuit responds to different frequencies applied
   to it.

**Frequency response curve**
   Graph of amplitude over frequency indicating a circuit response to different
   frequencies.

**Full wave rectifier**
   Rectifier that makes use of the full AC wave in both the positive and
   negative half cycles.

**Function generator**
   Signal generator that can produce sine, square, triangle and sawtooth
   output waveforms.

**Fundamental frequency**
   Lowest frequency in a complex waveform.

G
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Gain**
   Increase in voltage, current and/or power.

**Gain bandwidth product**
   Device parameter that indicates the maximum possible product of gain and
   bandwidth.

**GPIB**
   General-purpose instrument bus, also known as the IEEE-488 bus.

**Ground**
   Intentional or accidental conducting path between an electrical system
   or circuit and the earth.

H
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Half power point**
   Frequency at which the power is 50% of maximum (−3 dB point).

**h-parameters**
   Hybrid parameters; transistor specifications that describe the component
   operating limits.

**Half wave rectifier**
   Diode rectifier that converts AC to pulsating DC by passing only one
   half-cycle.

**Harmonic**
   Sine wave that is smaller in amplitude and an integer multiple of a
   fundamental frequency.

**Hole**
   Gap left in the covalent bond when a valence electron gains sufficient
   energy to become a free electron.

I
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**I²C**
   Inter-Integrated Circuit bus; short-distance serial communication bus
   standard.

**IC**
   Integrated circuit.

**IC voltage regulator**
   Three terminal device used to hold the output voltage of a power supply
   constant.

**IGFET**
   Insulated gate field effect transistor; another name for a MOSFET.

**Impedance (Z)**
   Measured in ohms; total opposition to the flow of current offered by a
   circuit, including both resistance and reactance.

**Interleave**
   Technique used in digitizing oscilloscopes whereby ADCs of different
   analog channels are used together to increase sample rate.

**Internal resistance**
   Every source has some resistance in series with the output current.

**Intrinsic material**
   Semiconductor material with electrical properties essentially characteristic
   of ideal pure crystal.

**Inverting amplifier**
   Amplifier that has a 180° phase shift from input to output.

**Inverting input**
   In an op-amp, the input that is marked with a minus sign.

**Ion**
   Atom with fewer or greater electrons in orbit than the number of protons
   in the nucleus.

J
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Junction**
   Contact or connection between two or more wires or cables.

**Junction diode**
   Semiconductor diode in which the rectifying characteristics occur at a
   junction between P-type and N-type materials.

K
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Kilo**
   Metric prefix for 1000 (10\ :sup:`3`).

**Kirchhoff's current law**
   The sum of the currents flowing into a point in a circuit is equal to
   the sum of the currents flowing out.

**Kirchhoff's voltage law**
   The algebraic sum of the voltage drops in a closed path circuit is equal
   to the algebraic sum of the source voltages.

**Knee voltage**
   Voltage at which a curve joins two relatively straight portions of a
   characteristic curve.

L
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**LC tank circuit**
   Circuit consisting of inductance and capacitance, capable of storing
   electrical energy and oscillating.

**Light-emitting diode (LED)**
   Semiconductor diode that converts electric energy into electromagnetic
   radiation (light).

**Limiter**
   Circuit or device that prevents some portion of its input from reaching
   the output.

**LIN**
   Local Interconnect Network; short-distance serial communication standard.

**Linear**
   Relationship between input and output in which the output varies in direct
   proportion to the input.

**Linear scale**
   Scale in which the divisions are uniformly spaced.

**Line regulation**
   Ability of a voltage regulator to maintain a constant voltage when the
   regulator input voltage varies.

**Load**
   Whatever component or piece of equipment is connected to a source and
   draws current.

**Load current**
   Current drawn from a source by a load.

**Load impedance**
   Vector sum of reactance and resistance in a load.

**Loading effect**
   Large load impedance will draw a small load current; small load impedance
   will draw a large load current.

**Load regulation**
   Ability of a voltage regulator to maintain a constant output voltage
   under varying load currents.

**Load resistance**
   Resistance of a load.

M
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Majority carriers**
   Conduction band electrons in an N-type material and valence band holes
   in a P-type material.

**Metal oxide field effect transistor (MOSFET)**
   FET in which the insulating layer between the gate electrode and the
   channel is a metal oxide layer.

**Mid-band gain**
   Gain of an amplifier operating within its bandwidth.

**Mid-point bias**
   Amplifier biased at the center of its DC load line.

**Mil**
   One thousandth of an inch (0.001 in.).

**Miller's theorem**
   Theorem that allows you to represent a feedback capacitor as equivalent
   input and output shunt capacitors.

**Minority carriers**
   Conduction band holes in N-type material and valence band electrons in
   P-type material.

**Mixed-signal oscilloscope (MSO)**
   Digitizing oscilloscope that combines analog and digital channels.

**Monostable**
   Circuit that has one stable state.

**MOSFET**
   Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field Effect Transistor; insulated gate field
   effect transistor.

**Multivibrator**
   Circuit used to implement a simple two-state system.

N
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**N-type semiconductor**
   Semiconductor compound formed by doping an intrinsic semiconductor with
   a pentavalent element.

**Negative**
   Terminal that has an excess of electrons.

**Negative charge**
   Charge that has more electrons than protons.

**Negative feedback**
   Feedback signal 180° out of phase with an amplifier input signal.

**Negative ion**
   Atom having a greater number of electrons in orbit than there are protons
   in the nucleus.

**Negative resistance**
   Resistance such that when the current through it increases the voltage
   drop across the resistance decreases.

**Negative temperature coefficient**
   Term describing a component whose resistance or capacitance decreases
   when temperature increases.

**Node**
   Junction or branch point in a circuit.

**Noise**
   Unwanted electromagnetic radiation within an electrical or mechanical
   system.

**Non-inverting input**
   Terminal on an operational amplifier that is identified by a plus sign.

**Non-linear scale**
   Scale in which the divisions are not equally spaced; logarithmic.

**Norton's theorem**
   Any network of voltage sources and resistors can be replaced by a single
   current source in parallel with a single resistor.

**NPN transistor**
   BJT in which a P-type base element is sandwiched between an N-type emitter
   and an N-type collector.

O
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**One-shot**
   Monostable circuit that produces one pulse when triggered.

**Open-loop gain**
   Gain obtained when no feedback is used in the circuit.

P
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Passive component**
   Component that does not amplify a signal.

**Peak to peak**
   Difference between the maximum positive and maximum negative values of
   an AC waveform.

**Pentavalent element**
   Element whose atoms have five valence electrons.

**Percent of regulation**
   Change in output voltage that occurs between no-load and full-load in
   a DC voltage source.

**Percent of ripple**
   Ratio of the effective RMS value of ripple voltage to the average value
   of the total voltage.

**Pinch-off region**
   Region on the characteristic curve of a FET in which the gate bias causes
   the depletion region to extend completely across the channel.

**PNP transistor**
   BJT with an N-type base and P-type emitter and collector.

**Positive feedback**
   Feedback signal that is in phase with an amplifier input signal.

**Potential difference**
   Voltage difference between two points which will cause current to flow.

**Potentiometer**
   Variable resistor with three terminals.

**Power supply rejection ratio (PSRR)**
   Measure of an op-amp's ability to maintain a constant output when the
   supply voltage varies.

**Protoboard**
   Board with provision for attaching components without solder.

Q
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Quiescent point (Q point)**
   DC operating point on the load line of a given amplifier.

R
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**RC time constant**
   Product of resistance and capacitance in seconds.

**Recombination**
   Process by which a conduction band electron gives up energy and falls
   back into a hole.

**Rectification**
   Process that converts alternating current to direct current.

**Rectifier**
   Diode circuit that converts alternating current into pulsating direct
   current.

**Regenerative feedback**
   Positive feedback; feedback from the output of an amplifier to the input
   that is in phase.

**Regulated power supply**
   Power supply that maintains a constant output voltage under changing
   load conditions.

**Regulator**
   Device or circuit that maintains a desired output under changing conditions.

**Resistance**
   Symbolized R and measured in ohms; opposition to current flow.

**Resistor**
   Component made of material that opposes flow of current.

**Reverse bias**
   Bias on a PN junction that allows only leakage current to flow.

**Reverse breakdown voltage**
   Amount of reverse bias that will cause a PN junction to break down.

**Reverse current**
   Current through a diode when reverse biased.

**Reverse saturation current**
   Reverse current through a diode caused by thermal activity.

**R-2R ladder**
   Network or circuit composed of a sequence of L networks connected in
   tandem, commonly used in DACs.

S
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Saturation**
   Condition in which a further increase in one variable produces no further
   increase in the resultant effect.

**Schematic diagram**
   Illustration of an electrical or electronic circuit using standard symbols.

**Scientific notation**
   Numbers expressed as a number from one to ten multiplied by a power of ten.

**SDRAM**
   Synchronous Dynamic Random-Access Memory.

**Self biasing**
   Gate bias for a FET in which source current through a resistor produces
   the voltage.

**Semiconductor**
   Element which is neither a good conductor nor a good insulator, but whose
   conductivity can be controlled.

**Series circuit**
   Circuit in which the components are connected end to end.

**Signal to noise ratio (SNR)**
   Ratio of the magnitude of the signal to the magnitude of noise.

**Silicon (Si)**
   Non-metallic element used in pure form as a semiconductor.

**Silicon dioxide**
   Glass-like material used as the gate insulating material in a MOSFET.

**Silicon transistor**
   BJT using silicon as the semiconducting material.

**Solid state**
   Pertaining to circuits where signals pass through solid semiconductor
   material.

**Source follower**
   FET amplifier in which signal is applied between gate and drain with
   output taken from the source.

**Source impedance**
   Impedance through which output current is taken from a source.

**Spectrum analyzer**
   Instrument used to display the frequency domain of a waveform.

**SPI**
   Serial Peripheral Interface; short-distance serial communication bus
   standard.

**Summing amplifier**
   Op-amp circuit whose output is proportional to the sum of its input
   voltages.

**Superposition theorem**
   In a network containing more than one source, the current at any one
   point is equal to the algebraic sum of the currents produced by each
   source acting separately.

T
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Thévenin's theorem**
   Theorem that replaces any complex network with a single voltage source
   in series with a single resistance.

**Threshold voltage**
   For an enhancement MOSFET, the minimum gate-source voltage required for
   conduction.

**Transconductance**
   Also called mutual conductance; ratio of a change in output current to
   the change in input voltage.

**Transistor**
   Term derived from "transfer resistor"; semiconductor device that can be
   used as an amplifier or as an electronic switch.

**Trivalent element**
   One having three valence electrons.

U
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Unity gain frequency**
   Frequency of operation for a device where the gain of the component drops
   to unity (1).

**USB**
   Universal Serial Bus; interface for connecting peripherals to computers.

V
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Valence shell**
   Outermost electron shell for a given atom.

**Varactor diode**
   PN junction diode with a high junction capacitance when reverse biased,
   used as a voltage-variable capacitor.

**Variable capacitor**
   Capacitor whose capacitance can be changed.

**Variable resistor**
   Resistor whose resistance can be changed by turning a shaft.

**Virtual ground**
   Point in a circuit that is always at approximately ground potential due
   to feedback.

**Volt**
   Unit of potential difference or electromotive force.

**Voltage (V)**
   Term used to designate electrical pressure or force that causes current
   to flow.

**Voltage amplifier**
   Amplifier designed to build up signal voltage.

**Voltage divider**
   Fixed or variable series resistor network connected across a voltage to
   produce a fraction of the input voltage.

**Voltage divider biasing**
   Biasing method used with amplifiers in which two series resistors are
   connected across a source.

**Voltage drop**
   Voltage or difference in potential developed across a component due to
   current flow.

**Voltage feedback**
   Feedback configuration where a portion of the output voltage is fed back
   to the input.

**Voltage follower**
   Op-amp circuit characterized by a high input impedance, low output
   impedance and unity voltage gain.

**Voltage gain**
   Ratio of amplifier output voltage to input voltage.

**Voltage multiplier**
   Rectifier circuit using diodes and capacitors to produce a DC output
   that is a multiple of the AC input peak.

**Voltage regulator**
   Device or circuit that maintains constant output voltage.

**Voltage source**
   Circuit or device that supplies voltage to a load.

W
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Watt**
   Unit of power equal to one joule per second.

Z
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**Zener diode**
   Semiconductor diode designed to operate in reverse breakdown, developing
   a constant voltage across its terminals.
