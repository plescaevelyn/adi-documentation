Beginner SDR Exercises

The following hands-on exercises demonstrate real-world software-defined radio applications using ADI’s SDR platforms. These exercises showcase the power of reconfigurable software running on specialized hardware, allowing you to implement complex signal processing algorithms without custom hardware design.

Note

The following exercises should be run on a Raspberry Pi 5 using the provided ADI Kuiper 2.0 Linux image. The Kuiper image contains all necessary software pre-installed, including Python, GNU Radio, Thonny IDE, and all required libraries. Connect your SDR hardware (Pluto, Jupiter, or Talise) to the Raspberry Pi 5 before starting the exercises.

Familiarize Yourself with the Tools

Before diving into the exercises, we recommend familiarizing yourself with the SDR hardware and tools you’ll be using. Understanding your hardware’s capabilities, features, and interfaces will help you get the most out of these exercises.

Taking time to understand your hardware will make the exercises more meaningful and help you troubleshoot issues more effectively.

Transmit and receive a complex sinusoid (Sinewave Loopback)

What is the easiest way to validate the basic functionalities of an SDR and analyze performance metrics? Add a loopback cable between TX and RX channels, transmit a complex sinusoid, and look at the spectrum on the receive side.

Through this basic test:

  • Verify if the up-conversion and down-conversion are accurate

  • See how clean the received spectrum is (Image Rejection, LO leakage, Spurs, SNR, SFDR)

  • Confirm that the software, HDL, and hardware work properly

  • Play with attributes to understand what “knobs” can be tweaked and how

  • Validate proper integration of the controls in your application

What is a Complex Sinusoid?

A complex sinusoid is a fundamental test signal in SDR systems, consisting of in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) components that create a single tone in the frequency domain. The complex exponential is represented as:

\[s(t) = A \cdot e^{j2\pi ft} = A \cdot (\cos(2\pi ft) + j\sin(2\pi ft))\]

where:

  • \(A\) = amplitude

  • \(f\) = frequency

  • \(t\) = time

  • Real part (I): \(A \cdot \cos(2\pi ft)\)

  • Quadrature part (Q): \(A \cdot \sin(2\pi ft)\)

In the constellation diagram, a complex sinusoid appears as a rotating point, tracing a circle around the origin. Its spectrum shows a single peak at the frequency \(f\).

Zero IF Architecture

Modern SDR platforms like Pluto, Jupiter, and Talise use a Zero Intermediate Frequency (Zero IF) architecture where the signal is directly converted to/from baseband without intermediate frequency stages.

The phase and frequency difference between TX and RX local oscillators affects Phase Modulation applications, requiring real-time phase and frequency correction algorithms.

Transmitter Chain:

  1. Digital Signal Generation: Create complex I/Q samples in software

  2. DAC (Digital-to-Analog Converter): Convert digital samples to analog voltages

  3. Reconstruction Filter: Remove high-frequency images from the DAC output

  4. I/Q Modulator: Mix I and Q signals with the Local Oscillator (LO) at 90° phase difference

  5. RF Amplifier: Amplify the upconverted signal to the desired transmit power

  6. Antenna: Radiate the RF signal

Receiver Chain:

  1. Antenna: Capture the RF signal

  2. RF Amplifier (LNA): Low-noise amplification

  3. I/Q Demodulator: Mix the RF signal with the LO to downconvert to baseband

  4. Anti-Aliasing Filter: Remove high-frequency content before sampling

  5. ADC (Analog-to-Digital Converter): Sample the analog I/Q signals

  6. Digital Processing: Process the received samples in software

Key Impairments to Observe

When analyzing the received spectrum, several impairments characterize real-world SDR performance:

1. Image (Mirror Signal):

Due to I/Q gain and phase imbalances in the mixer, a mirror image of the desired signal appears on the opposite side of the LO frequency. The Image Rejection Ratio (IRR) quantifies how well the system suppresses this unwanted image:

\[\text{IRR (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{wanted}}}{P_{\text{image}}}\right)\]

Causes of poor image rejection:

  • Gain Imbalance: I and Q channels have different amplification (should be identical)

  • Phase Imbalance: I and Q mixer phases deviate from the ideal 90° quadrature relationship

2. LO Leakage (DC Offset):

Local Oscillator leakage occurs when the LO signal couples into the mixer output, creating a DC offset in the baseband I/Q signals. This appears as a spike at the center frequency (DC in baseband).

Causes:

  • Imperfect mixer isolation

  • DC offsets in the analog signal chain

  • LO signal leaking through the RF path

3. Spurs (Spurious Signals):

Unwanted tones that appear at predictable frequency offsets due to:

  • Clock harmonics

  • Intermodulation products

  • Switching noise from power supplies

  • Digital switching transients coupling into analog paths

4. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):

The SNR measures the quality of the received signal relative to the noise floor:

\[\text{SNR (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{signal}}}{P_{\text{noise}}}\right)\]

Higher SNR indicates cleaner signal reception and better ADC/DAC performance.

Using PyADI-IIO with Pluto

The Python script performs the following steps:

  1. Configure SDR - Set up the radio parameters (sample rate, center frequency, gain, etc.)

  2. Create a discrete complex sinusoid - Generate the I/Q samples mathematically

  3. Call tx() to transmit - Send the created sinusoid as I and Q data

  4. Call rx() to receive - Capture the I and Q data from the receiver

  5. Plot the received sinusoid - Visualize the results

1. Configure SDR:

 1import adi
 2import numpy as np
 3
 4sdr = adi.Pluto("ip:pluto.local")  # Create Radio object
 5
 6sample_rate = 3000000  # Sample rate in Hz
 7center_freq = 915000000  # Center frequency in Hz
 8num_samps = 100000  # Number of samples
 9
10# Configure TX
11sdr.tx_lo = center_freq
12sdr.tx_hardwaregain_chan0 = -10  # TX attenuation in dB
13sdr.tx_cyclic_buffer = True
14
15# Configure RX
16sdr.rx_lo = center_freq
17sdr.sample_rate = sample_rate
18sdr.rx_rf_bandwidth = sample_rate
19sdr.rx_buffer_size = num_samps
20sdr.gain_control_mode_chan0 = "slow_attack"

2. Create a discrete complex sinusoid:

25frequency = 20000  # 20 kHz sinusoid
26fs = int(sdr.sample_rate)
27N = num_samps
28t = np.arange(N) / fs
29samples = 0.5 * np.exp(2.0j * np.pi * frequency * t)

3. Call tx() to transmit:

Using PyADI-IIO, only this function needs to be called to start transmitting:

31sdr.tx(samples)

4. Call rx() to receive:

Only this function needs to be called to receive:

33rx_samples = sdr.rx()

If you run the example multiple times, the RX capture will have a different phase shift compared with TX each time. Even if the LO is the same, there are frequency dividers on each channel with random phase shift for each run.

  2. Open the terminal by simultaneously pressing Ctrl + Alt + T on your keyboard.

Binary Phase Shift Keying (BPSK)

Binary Phase Shift Keying is the simplest type of Phase Modulation where the binary data (bits 0 and 1) are encoded using two distinct phase states of the carrier.

Encoding: bits → symbols × carrier_sinusoid

  • bit 0 → symbol -1+0j

  • bit 1 → symbol +1+0j

For BPSK, Q = 0 (all energy in the In-phase component).

Where is Phase Modulation used?

  • GSM

  • Satellite Television

  • Wi-Fi

  • Many others

Ideal World vs Real World

In an ideal world where the LOs of TX and RX are perfectly in sync and where there is no time delay between RX and TX: in = out

In a real world: in != out but the information is still there.

What are the problems when demodulating PSK signals?

  • Phase offset of LO at RX relative to TX

  • Frequency offset of LO at RX relative to TX

  • Variation of these two in time with distance change and temperature change

Luckily, all these can be solved by software!

Differential Encoding and Decoding

Differential encoding solves the phase ambiguity problem:

  • To transmit a bit of “1”: change state (if previous is “0”, change to “1”; if previous is “1”, change to “0”)

  • To transmit a bit of “0”: repeat state (if previous is “1”, repeat “1”; if previous is “0”, repeat “0”)

How the Python Script is Structured

Transmitter:

  1. Configure SDR

  2. Create array of bits

  3. Differential Encoding

  4. Interpolate with 16 sps and Remap symbols: bit 0 → -1, bit 1 → 1

  5. Shift Spectrum

  6. Call tx() function and transmit

Receiver:

  1. Call rx() function to receive data

  2. Adjust the frequency offset (coarse)

  3. Select the right samples and decimate (Symbol Sync)

  4. Fine frequency and phase adjustment (Costas Loop)

  5. Differential Decoding

  6. Plot data

Coarse Frequency Offset Adjustment

How to adjust the frequency offset:

  1. First square the received signal → all symbols (s(t))² will have a constant positive value

  2. Take the FFT and measure the peak → the measured peak will be at 2×offset_frequency

  3. Apply frequency correction on received samples based on the above measurement

Symbol Timing Recovery (Mueller and Muller)

How to select the right samples (Mueller and Muller timing recovery technique):

The variable “mu” represents the timing offset to add to 16sps because we have to select one from each 16 samples. For example, if mu = 2.43 → we have to shift the input by 2.43 samples. After a few iterations of the while loop, “mu” stabilizes and only the correct samples are pulled.

We want to sample where the adjacent symbols cross 0 and discard in-between samples.

Fine Frequency Synchronization (Costas Loop)

The Costas Loop functions like a PLL. We multiply the real part of the sample (I) by the imaginary part (Q), and because Q should be equal to zero for BPSK, the error function is minimized when there is no phase or frequency offset that causes energy to shift from I to Q.

Q is the error signal - adjust and keep Q = 0.

The Python script performs the following steps:

Transmitter - Create bits and encode:

1# Create the array of bits and encode them with Differential Encoding
2num_symbols = 1000
3bits = np.random.randint(0, 2, num_symbols)
4
5# Differential encoding
6encoded_bits = np.zeros(num_symbols, dtype=int)
7encoded_bits[0] = bits[0]
8for i in range(1, num_symbols):
9    encoded_bits[i] = encoded_bits[i-1] ^ bits[i]

Transmitter - Interpolate and shift spectrum:

12# Repeat each bit 16 times (interpolate) => 16sps
13sps = 16
14samples = np.repeat(encoded_bits * 2 - 1, sps).astype(np.complex64)
15
16# For BPSK the data is complex (Q = 0)
17# Shift spectrum to a higher frequency
18f_offset = 100000  # 100 kHz offset
19t = np.arange(len(samples)) / sample_rate
20samples = samples * np.exp(2.0j * np.pi * f_offset * t)
21
22# Transmit
23sdr.tx(samples)

Receiver - Coarse frequency adjustment:

25# Receive samples
26rx_samples = sdr.rx()
27
28# Square the signal to find frequency offset
29squared = rx_samples ** 2
30fft_squared = np.fft.fftshift(np.fft.fft(squared))
31freqs = np.fft.fftshift(np.fft.fftfreq(len(squared), 1/sample_rate))
32max_freq = freqs[np.argmax(np.abs(fft_squared))]
33offset_freq = max_freq / 2  # Divide by 2 because we squared
34
35# Apply frequency correction
36t = np.arange(len(rx_samples)) / sample_rate
37rx_corrected = rx_samples * np.exp(-2.0j * np.pi * offset_freq * t)

Spectrum Paint using Pluto

This exercise demonstrates a creative application of SDR: “painting” images in the frequency spectrum. By transmitting signals at different frequencies over time, you can create visible patterns on a waterfall display, which shows frequency on the horizontal axis and time on the vertical axis.

How Spectrum Paint Works:

A waterfall display is a spectrogram that shows:

  • Frequency (horizontal axis): Different colors represent signal strength at each frequency

  • Time (vertical axis): Each new row represents a new time snapshot

  • Amplitude (color intensity): Brighter colors indicate stronger signals

By carefully controlling which frequencies are transmitted at each moment, you can “draw” images, text, or patterns that become visible as the waterfall scrolls down.

Applications:

While spectrum paint is primarily a demonstration, the underlying technique is used in:

  • Frequency-hopping spread spectrum (FHSS)

  • Chirp radar systems

  • Multi-carrier communications

  • Signal identification and watermarking

This exercise showcases the flexibility of software-defined radio: the same hardware can transmit any arbitrary frequency pattern by simply changing the software.
