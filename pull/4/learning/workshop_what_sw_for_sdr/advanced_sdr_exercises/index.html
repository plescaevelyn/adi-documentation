These exercises build upon the fundamentals covered in the beginner exercises and explore more sophisticated signal processing techniques, multi-channel systems, and real-world communication protocols.

Note

These advanced exercises require a solid understanding of SDR fundamentals. If you’re new to SDR, we recommend starting with the Beginner SDR Exercises before attempting these advanced topics.

Note

The following exercises should be run on a Raspberry Pi 5 using the provided ADI Kuiper 2.0 Linux image. The Kuiper image contains all necessary software pre-installed, including Python, GNU Radio, Scopy, and all required libraries. Connect your SDR hardware (Pluto, Jupiter, or Talise) to the Raspberry Pi 5 before starting the exercises. Use antennas or loopback cables as shown in the exercise diagrams.

What Separates Beginners from Advanced SDR Users:

Learning to test your SDR and find mistakes on your own is the critical step that differentiates a beginner from an advanced-level SDR user. While beginners focus on running predefined exercises and observing expected results, advanced users develop the skills to validate system performance, identify impairments, and troubleshoot issues independently. The following exercises build these advanced capabilities.

First Steps in SDR Testing

This exercise teaches you how to validate SDR performance and identify common impairments by transmitting a complex sinusoid through a loopback connection and analyzing key metrics. Understanding how to test and characterize your SDR is fundamental to developing robust wireless systems.

Why Testing Matters:

Before deploying any wireless communication system, you must validate that your SDR hardware and software are functioning correctly. This exercise demonstrates how to:

  • Verify that up/down conversion works accurately

  • Check spectrum cleanliness and identify impairments

  • Validate that software, HDL (Hardware Description Language), and hardware are working together properly

  • Understand the impact of attribute tweaking on performance

  • Ensure proper control integration between software and hardware

What is a Complex Sinusoid?

A complex sinusoid is a fundamental test signal in SDR systems, consisting of in-phase (I) and quadrature (Q) components that create a single tone in the frequency domain. As covered in the Transmit and receive a complex sinusoid (Sinewave Loopback) beginner exercise, the complex exponential is represented as:

\[s(t) = A \cdot e^{j2\pi ft} = A \cdot (\cos(2\pi ft) + j\sin(2\pi ft))\]

where:

  • \(A\) = amplitude

  • \(f\) = frequency

  • \(t\) = time

  • Real part (I): \(A \cdot \cos(2\pi ft)\)

  • Quadrature part (Q): \(A \cdot \sin(2\pi ft)\)

In the constellation diagram, a complex sinusoid appears as a rotating point, tracing a circle around the origin. Its spectrum shows a single peak at the frequency \(f\).

Zero IF Architecture:

Modern SDR platforms like Pluto, Jupiter, and Talise use a Zero Intermediate Frequency (Zero IF) architecture where the signal is directly converted to/from baseband without intermediate frequency stages.

Transmitter Chain:

  1. Digital Signal Generation: Create complex I/Q samples in software

  2. DAC (Digital-to-Analog Converter): Convert digital samples to analog voltages

  3. Reconstruction Filter: Remove high-frequency images from the DAC output

  4. I/Q Modulator: Mix I and Q signals with the Local Oscillator (LO) at 90° phase difference

  5. RF Amplifier: Amplify the upconverted signal to the desired transmit power

  6. Antenna: Radiate the RF signal

Receiver Chain:

  1. Antenna: Capture the RF signal

  2. RF Amplifier (LNA): Low-noise amplification

  3. I/Q Demodulator: Mix the RF signal with the LO to downconvert to baseband

  4. Anti-Aliasing Filter: Remove high-frequency content before sampling

  5. ADC (Analog-to-Digital Converter): Sample the analog I/Q signals

  6. Digital Processing: Process the received samples in software

Key Impairments to Observe:

When analyzing the received spectrum, you will observe several impairments that characterize real-world SDR performance:

1. Image (Mirror Signal):

Due to I/Q gain and phase imbalances in the mixer, a mirror image of the desired signal appears on the opposite side of the LO frequency. The Image Rejection Ratio (IRR) quantifies how well the system suppresses this unwanted image:

\[\text{IRR (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{wanted}}}{P_{\text{image}}}\right)\]

Causes of Poor Image Rejection:

  • Gain Imbalance: I and Q channels have different amplification (should be identical)

  • Phase Imbalance: I and Q mixer phases deviate from the ideal 90° quadrature relationship

Better SDR hardware achieves higher IRR values (40-60 dB typical for high-quality systems).

2. LO Leakage (DC Offset):

Local Oscillator leakage occurs when the LO signal couples into the mixer output, creating a DC offset in the baseband I/Q signals. This appears as a spike at the center frequency (DC in baseband).

Causes:

  • Imperfect mixer isolation

  • DC offsets in the analog signal chain

  • LO signal leaking through the RF path

3. Spurs (Spurious Signals):

Unwanted tones that appear at predictable frequency offsets due to:

  • Clock harmonics

  • Intermodulation products

  • Switching noise from power supplies

  • Digital switching transients coupling into analog paths

4. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR):

The SNR measures the quality of the received signal relative to the noise floor:

\[\text{SNR (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\text{signal}}}{P_{\text{noise}}}\right)\]

Higher SNR indicates cleaner signal reception and better ADC/DAC performance.

What You’ll Observe in This Exercise:

By transmitting a known complex sinusoid through a loopback cable and analyzing the received spectrum, you will:

  • Measure SNR: Quantify how much the signal stands above the noise floor

  • Identify Image Rejection: See the mirror image and calculate IRR

  • Observe LO Leakage: Identify the DC offset spike at the center

  • Detect Spurs: Find spurious tones and correlate them with system clocks

  • Validate Frequency Accuracy: Confirm that transmitted and received frequencies match

This systematic testing approach forms the foundation for troubleshooting more complex communication systems.

Using Scopy for Visual Analysis

While PyADI-IIO provides programmatic control and testing, Scopy offers a powerful visual interface for real-time signal analysis and debugging.

Scopy with ADI SDR Platforms:

Scopy is ADI’s comprehensive software toolset for signal analysis and controlling IIO (Industrial I/O) systems. It provides full support for ADI SDR platforms including:

  • ADALM-PLUTO: Standard and modified (Rev C) with TX2/RX2 access

  • AD-JUPITER-EBZ (Jupiter): Full 2×2 MIMO support

  • ADRV9009-ZU11EG (Talise): Production-grade evaluation platform

For complete documentation on Scopy’s features, architecture, and installation, see the Scopy documentation.

Using Scopy for SDR Testing:

In this exercise, you’ll use Scopy’s instruments to visually analyze SDR impairments and performance metrics in real-time, making it easy to identify issues and validate hardware performance without writing code.

Follow these steps to perform visual SDR analysis with Scopy:

  1. Make the loopback setup using Pluto with TX1 connected to RX1 via an SMA loopback cable and connect it to your PC.

  2. Launch Scopy from your applications menu or terminal:

    analog@analog:~$

    • Click Connect

    • Open the AD9361 plugin (AD936X)

    • Set RX LO Frequency: 2450000000 Hz and press Enter

    • Set TX LO Frequency: 2450000000 Hz and press Enter

    • Set RX1 Gain: 0 dB

    • Set RX2 Gain: 0 dB (unused channel)

    • Set TX1 Attenuation: -10 dB

    • Set TX2 Attenuation: -80 dB (unused channel - disable it)

  5. Configure the DDS (Direct Digital Synthesizer) to generate a test tone:

    • Open the DDS tab in the AD9361 plugin

    • Set Frequency: 89000 Hz (baseband tone) and press Enter

    • Set Amplitude: 0.9 (scale factor)

    • Set Phase: 90 degrees

    • Enable TX1 DDS output

  6. Configure the Data Logger (Oscilloscope/Spectrum Analyzer):

    • Open the Data Logger instrument

    • Set Sample Rate: 30720000 SPS (samples per second)

    • Enable FFT view for spectrum analysis

    • IMPORTANT: Check the Y-axis scale setting

      • If the default scale shows -Inf (negative infinity), you need to change it

      • Look for the scale settings (usually in the display options or Y-axis configuration)

      • Set an appropriate scale (e.g., -120 dB to 0 dB for spectrum view)

      • Red rectangle indicator: The scale setting is typically found in the top-right corner of the plot area

  7. Observe the time domain signals:

    • I channel (Real): Cosine waveform at 89000 Hz

    • Q channel (Imaginary): Sine waveform at 89000 Hz (90° phase offset due to DDS phase setting)

    • Verify both signals have the same amplitude

    • Check for any DC offset or amplitude imbalance

  8. Analyze the frequency domain (FFT):

    • Switch to Spectrum Analyzer view or enable FFT overlay in the Data Logger

    • Identify the wanted signal peak at +89000 Hz

    • Identify the image signal (mirror) at -89000 Hz

    • Observe LO leakage at DC (0 Hz)

    • Detect any spurious tones (spurs)

    • Measure the noise floor

    • Cursors: Place cursors on wanted (at +89 kHz) and image signals (at -89 kHz) to measure power difference (IRR)

    • Markers: Identify peak frequencies and amplitudes

    • Statistics: View min/max/average/RMS values

    • SNR Calculator: Automatic SNR measurement (if available in your Scopy version)

    Example measurements you might observe:

    • Image signal power: ~-45 dBFS (depends on I/Q balance)

    • Image Rejection Ratio (IRR): Example: -10 - (-45) = 35 dB

    • Noise floor: ~-90 dBFS (depends on sample rate and ADC performance)

    • SNR: Example: -10 - (-90) = 80 dB

  10. Experiment with Genalyzer integration (if available):

    • Open the Genalyzer plugin

    • Perform advanced spectral analysis

    • Calculate metrics: SINAD, THD, SFDR, ENOB

    • Export data for further analysis

  11. Advanced debugging with Register Map:

    • Open the Register Map tool

    • Inspect RF transceiver registers

    • Verify LO frequency settings

    • Check gain control settings

    • Modify parameters and observe real-time effects

    • Real-time visual feedback

Debugging and Tweaking IIO Attributes

This advanced exercise teaches you how to interact with the Industrial I/O (IIO) subsystem at a low level, giving you direct control over hardware attributes. Understanding IIO attributes is essential for debugging SDR issues, optimizing performance, and integrating SDR control into custom applications.

What are IIO Attributes?

IIO attributes are the low-level parameters that control every aspect of your SDR hardware:

  • Channel attributes: Per-channel settings like gain, frequency, sample rate

  • Device attributes: Global settings affecting the entire device

  • Debug attributes: Low-level register access for advanced debugging

Every high-level API (PyADI-IIO, GNU Radio, MATLAB, Scopy) ultimately reads and writes these IIO attributes through the Linux kernel. By learning to manipulate them directly, you gain:

  • Debugging skills: Identify whether issues are in your application or the hardware layer

  • Fine-grained control: Access parameters not exposed by high-level APIs

  • Integration knowledge: Understand how to integrate SDR control into any application

  • Troubleshooting ability: Verify attribute values when behavior seems unexpected

  1. PyADI-IIO: Python library for SDR control

Understanding the IIO Naming Convention:

IIO attribute names follow a pattern:

  • Direction: in_ (input/RX) or out_ (output/TX)

  • Channel type: voltage, altvoltage, temp, etc.

  • Channel number: 0, 1, 2, etc.

  • Attribute name: hardwaregain, sampling_frequency, rf_bandwidth, etc.

Examples:

  • in_voltage0_hardwaregain: RX1 channel gain (input voltage channel 0)

  • out_voltage0_hardwaregain: TX1 channel attenuation (output voltage channel 0)

  • out_altvoltage0_RX_LO_frequency: RX local oscillator frequency

  • in_voltage_sampling_frequency: Global RX sample rate (applies to all RX channels)

Digital QAM Modulation: 16QAM Loopback with EVM Measurements

This advanced exercise explores 16-QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation), a higher-order modulation scheme that encodes 4 bits per symbol by varying both phase and amplitude. Unlike BPSK (1 bit/symbol) or QPSK (2 bits/symbol), 16QAM uses 16 distinct constellation points, achieving higher spectral efficiency at the cost of requiring better signal quality.

Why 16QAM Matters:

16QAM is widely used in modern wireless standards:

  • 4G LTE: Downlink modulation for medium-to-high SNR conditions

  • 5G NR: One of the baseline modulation schemes

  • Wi-Fi (802.11ac/ax): High-throughput data transmission

  • DVB-T2: Digital television broadcasting

  • Microwave backhaul: Point-to-point links

This exercise introduces Error Vector Magnitude (EVM) measurements, a critical metric for evaluating digital modulation quality in production systems.

What is EVM?

Error Vector Magnitude quantifies how much the received constellation points deviate from their ideal positions. It’s the industry-standard metric for measuring transmitter and receiver quality.

\[\text{EVM}_{\text{RMS}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{|S_{\text{measured},i} - S_{\text{ideal},i}|^2}{|S_{\text{ideal},i}|^2}} \times 100\%\]

where:

  • \(S_{\text{measured},i}\) = received symbol

  • \(S_{\text{ideal},i}\) = ideal constellation point

  • \(N\) = number of symbols

Lower EVM = better signal quality. Typical requirements:

  • 4G LTE: EVM < 8% for 16QAM

  • 5G NR: EVM < 7% for 16QAM

  • Wi-Fi 6: EVM < 6.5% for 16QAM

16QAM Constellation:

16QAM uses a 4×4 grid constellation with 16 points:

  • 4 amplitude levels: Each axis (I and Q) has 4 discrete levels

  • 4 bits per symbol: 2^4 = 16 possible symbols

  • Higher spectral efficiency: 2× more bits per symbol than QPSK

  • Higher SNR requirement: More sensitive to noise and impairments

Key Difference from QPSK:

  • QPSK: All symbols have the same amplitude, only phase changes

  • 16QAM: Symbols have different amplitudes AND phases

  • Implication: 16QAM is more sensitive to amplitude distortion (compression, non-linearity)

Understanding Burst Mode Transmission:

This exercise uses burst mode instead of continuous transmission:

  • Burst mode:

    • Data sent in packets with idle periods between bursts

    • More spectrum-efficient (transmit only when you have data)

    • Harder to demodulate (loops don’t have time to fully lock)

    • Realistic for packet-based protocols (Wi-Fi, LTE, etc.)

  • Continuous mode:

    • Always transmitting (even if just noise/padding)

    • Easier to demodulate (loops stay locked)

    • Less realistic for real systems

Why MIMO Matters:

MIMO is fundamental to modern wireless systems:

  • 4G LTE: 2×2 or 4×4 MIMO for higher throughput

  • 5G NR: Up to 8×8 MIMO in sub-6 GHz, massive MIMO in mmWave

  • Wi-Fi 6/7: Multi-user MIMO (MU-MIMO) for simultaneous client connections

  • Beamforming: Directional signal transmission for better range and interference rejection

  • Spatial multiplexing: Transmit independent data streams on each antenna for higher data rates

AD9361 2×2 MIMO Architecture:

The AD9361 is a 2×2 transceiver with:

  • 2 independent TX channels: TX1 and TX2

  • 2 independent RX channels: RX1 and RX2

  • Shared LO: Both channels use the same local oscillator (phase-coherent)

  • Independent gain/phase control: Each channel can be tuned separately

  • Baseband I/Q: Each channel outputs complex I/Q samples

Deep Dive: Symbol Synchronization for QPSK

This advanced exercise isolates Symbol Synchronization (also called symbol timing recovery), a critical DSP block that aligns the receiver’s sampling clock with the transmitter’s symbol boundaries. Understanding symbol sync is essential for implementing any digital modulation scheme.

The Synchronization Problem:

When receiving a digital signal, the receiver faces several challenges:

  1. Frequency offset: TX and RX local oscillators don’t match exactly (handled by FLL)

  2. Phase offset: Carrier phase is unknown (handled by Costas Loop)

  3. Timing offset: Don’t know WHEN symbols start/end (handled by Symbol Sync)

  4. Sample rate mismatch: TX and RX clocks drift over time

This exercise focuses on timing synchronization - the problem of determining the optimal sampling instant for each symbol.

Why Symbol Sync Matters:

  • Without symbol sync: Sampling at random times within symbol periods → high error rate

  • With symbol sync: Sampling at optimal decision points → maximum eye opening, minimal ISI

What Symbol Sync Does:

The Symbol Sync block:

  1. Estimates symbol timing: Finds when symbols start and end

  2. Resamples the signal: Outputs exactly 1 sample per symbol (SPS = 1)

  3. Tracks timing drift: Adapts to clock frequency differences between TX and RX

  4. Applies matched filtering: Often includes RRC filter for optimal SNR

Key Parameters:

  • Samples per symbol (SPS): Input has multiple samples per symbol (e.g., 4 SPS), output has 1 SPS

  • Loop bandwidth: Controls how quickly timing adjusts (trade-off: fast acquisition vs noise immunity)

  • Damping factor: Controls oscillation behavior during lock acquisition

  • Timing error detector: Algorithm that estimates sampling phase error (Mueller-Müller, Gardner, etc.)

Understanding the Symbol Sync Block:

The GNU Radio Symbol Sync block (or Polyphase Clock Sync) implements:

  1. Timing error detector: Estimates whether sampling is early or late

    • Mueller-Müller algorithm: Common for PSK modulations

    • Gardner algorithm: Non-data-aided, works without knowing symbol values

    • Early-Late Gate: Simple but effective for pulse-shaped signals

  2. Loop filter: Smooths timing error estimates

    • Low-pass filters noisy error signals

    • Determines loop bandwidth and damping

  3. Interpolator: Resamples signal at optimal timing instants

    • Polyphase filterbank: Efficiently resamples with arbitrary fractional delay

    • Includes matched filter (RRC) for optimal SNR

  4. Timing drift tracking: Continuously adapts to TX/RX clock frequency differences

Timing Error Detector Intuition:

The Mueller-Müller algorithm uses this insight:

  • At optimal sampling instant: Symbol transitions are abrupt

  • Early sampling: Still in previous symbol (transition not complete)

  • Late sampling: Already in next symbol (transition overshot)

By analyzing consecutive samples and their differences, the algorithm estimates timing error and adjusts the sampling phase.

Loop Parameters:

  • Loop bandwidth: Typically 0.01 to 0.1 rad/sample

    • Higher BW: Faster acquisition, more sensitive to noise

    • Lower BW: Slower acquisition, more robust to noise

  • Damping factor: Typically 0.707 (critically damped)

    • Higher damping: Less overshoot, slower settling

    • Lower damping: Faster settling, more overshoot

Deep Dive: Carrier Synchronization with Costas Loop

This advanced exercise isolates the Costas Loop, a phase-locked loop (PLL) that performs carrier synchronization by removing residual frequency and phase offsets. The Costas Loop is the final synchronization stage in a complete QPSK receiver, enabling accurate symbol demodulation.

The Carrier Synchronization Problem:

Even after FLL (coarse frequency correction) and Symbol Sync (timing recovery), the received constellation still has:

  • Residual frequency offset: FLL corrects to within ~100 Hz, but not perfectly

  • Phase offset: Unknown carrier phase (could be rotated 0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°)

  • Phase drift: TX and RX oscillators drift slowly over time

Without carrier synchronization:

  • Constellation rotates continuously (frequency offset)

  • Constellation has fixed but unknown rotation (phase offset)

  • Cannot make correct symbol decisions

What is a Costas Loop?

The Costas Loop is a carrier recovery circuit that:

  1. Estimates phase error: Computes how much the constellation is rotated

  2. Generates correction: Produces a phase offset to counteract the rotation

  3. Tracks phase drift: Continuously adapts to changing carrier phase

It’s specifically designed for suppressed-carrier modulations (BPSK, QPSK, 8PSK) where there’s no pilot tone to lock onto.

Why “Costas” Loop?

Named after John P. Costas (1956), who invented this technique for carrier recovery without a pilot tone. Before Costas, receivers needed the transmitter to send a separate carrier reference signal. The Costas Loop extracts carrier phase information from the modulated signal itself.

Costas Loop Architecture:

Key Components:

  1. Phase error detector: Computes error = I × Q (for QPSK)

    • When constellation is correctly aligned: I × Q ≈ 0

    • When rotated: I × Q ≠ 0, sign indicates direction

  2. Loop filter: Low-pass filters the error signal

    • Smooths noisy error estimates

    • Determines loop bandwidth (trade-off: acquisition speed vs jitter)

  3. NCO (Numerically Controlled Oscillator): Generates phase correction

    • Integrates filtered error to produce phase offset

    • Rotates the constellation to cancel carrier offset

  4. Complex multiplier: Applies phase correction to input signal

Phase Error Detector for QPSK:

The classic Costas error function for QPSK is:

\[\text{error} = \text{sign}(I) \times Q - \text{sign}(Q) \times I\]

This is a decision-directed error detector:

  • Uses hard decisions (sign) on in-phase component

  • Measures error on quadrature component

  • Minimized when constellation aligns with decision boundaries

Understanding Costas Loop Parameters:

The GNU Radio Costas Loop block has these key parameters:

  • Loop bandwidth: Controls how aggressively the loop tracks phase changes

    • High BW (>0.05): Fast acquisition, good for high dynamics, but more sensitive to noise

    • Low BW (<0.01): Slow acquisition, rejects noise well, but can’t track fast phase changes

  • Order: QPSK uses order=4 (4-PSK)

    • Order = number of constellation points with equal magnitude

    • BPSK = 2, QPSK = 4, 8PSK = 8

  • Maximum frequency deviation: How much frequency offset the loop can track

Costas Loop vs PLL:

  • Traditional PLL: Needs a pilot tone (unmodulated carrier)

  • Costas Loop: Recovers carrier from modulated signal (no pilot needed)

The Costas Loop is essentially a data-aided PLL that uses symbol structure to estimate phase error.

Phase Error Detector Deep Dive:

For QPSK, the error function is:

error = I * sign(Q) - Q * sign(I)

Intuition:

  • When constellation is aligned (I and Q at ±1): error ≈ 0

  • When rotated clockwise: error > 0 → NCO compensates counterclockwise

  • When rotated counterclockwise: error < 0 → NCO compensates clockwise

The sign() function makes it decision-directed: it assumes symbols are near decision boundaries and uses that assumption to compute error.

Complete QPSK Receiver Synchronization Chain:

You’ve now studied all three synchronization stages:

  1. FLL (Frequency-Locked Loop):

    • Function: Coarse frequency offset correction

    • Accuracy: ±100 Hz

    • Speed: Fast acquisition

  2. Symbol Sync:

    • Function: Timing recovery + matched filtering

    • Output: 1 sample per symbol at optimal decision instants

    • Adapts to: Sample clock differences between TX and RX

  3. Costas Loop:

    • Function: Fine frequency and phase correction

    • Accuracy: <1 Hz frequency error, <5° phase error

    • Resolves: Residual offsets and drift

Together, these blocks form a complete carrier and timing synchronization subsystem that enables reliable QPSK demodulation under realistic channel conditions.
